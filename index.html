The Problem
Clarity Canvas

Complete this before you look at a single vendor. AI is a category of solution — not an answer in itself. This canvas defines the problem you're actually trying to solve and helps you decide whether AI is the right approach.

Use this when
You're considering AI investment, responding to a vendor pitch, or building an internal business case.
Use before Card 2 (Evidence Hierarchy) and Card 3 (Vendor Rubric)
The most common AI mistake in behavioral health: selecting a tool before defining the problem. Vendors define the problem for you — usually in a way their product solves. This canvas reverses that. Answer these questions with your leadership team before any vendor enters the room.
1
Name the operational problem
What is actually breaking, slowing down, or failing in your organization? Be specific — not "documentation burden" but "clinicians spend 2+ hrs/day on notes, contributing to our 22% turnover rate."
Write the problem here in one or two sentences...
2
Quantify the impact
What does this problem cost in measurable terms? Consider: staff hours lost, turnover costs, patient access delayed, outcomes not measured, revenue not captured.
What does it cost per month / per year?
3
Trace the root cause
Ask "why" three times. Is the root cause a process failure, a staffing gap, a data infrastructure problem, a policy gap, or a workflow design issue? AI solves some of these — not all of them.
Root cause (not symptom)...
4
Define success
What would "solved" look like in 12 months? Use measurable terms: "Clinician documentation time reduced by 40%," "7-day follow-up rate above 85%," "Intake wait time under 72 hours."
What does success look like?
5
Audit existing solutions — what have you already tried?
Non-AI solutions often solve operational problems just as effectively — and with less governance risk. List what's already been tried, and why it hasn't been enough.
What process changes, hires, or tools have already been attempted?
→
The AI decision gate: which path does your problem fall into?
Green — Proceed to Card 2
AI is the right category
The problem involves scale, pattern recognition, or automation at a volume humans can't match. The root cause is not a process or staffing gap that could be solved another way. Clinical evidence for AI in this use case exists.
Amber — Pause and investigate
AI might help, but clarify first
The problem could be solved with better process design, a targeted hire, or improved data infrastructure. AI may amplify the problem rather than solve it. Get clearer on root cause before proceeding.
Red — Do not proceed to vendor evaluation
AI is not the right solution
The root cause is a workflow, staffing, leadership, or culture problem. AI will not fix it and will add governance risk. Address the underlying issue first — then revisit AI when the foundation is stronger.

The Clinical
Evidence Hierarchy

Only 32.8% of AI therapy tools incorporate established psychotherapy theory (Iftikhar et al., AIES 2025). "Evidence-based" means different things to different vendors. This card tells you exactly what to look for — and what questions to ask before any tool enters a patient pathway.

Use this when
A vendor presents their AI tool. You're reviewing a contract or proposal. Your clinical team is evaluating options.
Use after Card 1. Use before Card 3 (Vendor Rubric).
The procurement literacy gap: Most behavioral health executives cannot distinguish between Level 1 and Level 5 evidence — and vendors know it. This hierarchy gives you the vocabulary to ask harder questions. A published blog post and a pre-registered RCT in Nature Medicine are not the same thing. Your patients deserve to know the difference.
5
Pre-registered RCT with blinded outcome assessment
Gold standard
Study design registered before data collection. Outcomes assessed by evaluators blinded to treatment group. Published in peer-reviewed clinical journal. Results replicated across settings.
Example: Limbic Layer study, Nature Medicine, March 2026 — 227 participants, blinded CBT scoring by independent clinicians, pre-registered design.
4
Peer-reviewed observational study with large real-world dataset
Strong
Published in peer-reviewed journal. Large sample size from real clinical deployment (not pilot). Validated clinical outcome measures used. Transparent methodology. No blinding, but no conflicts of interest in study design.
Example: Limbic Access real-world study, 64,862 patients across 9 NHS Talking Therapies sites, published outcomes including recovery rates and wait time reductions.
3
Peer-reviewed pilot study or conference paper
Moderate
Published in peer-reviewed journal or presented at academic conference with review process. Smaller sample size or limited to one site. Results promising but not yet generalizable. Clinical measures used, but not independently validated.
Sufficient for consideration — not sufficient for widespread deployment without additional validation in your specific patient population.
2
Internal case study or white paper
Weak
Vendor-produced document describing outcomes at a partner organization. No independent review. Outcome measures may be self-selected. Sample may not be representative. Conflicts of interest not disclosed. Useful for understanding use cases — not for clinical validation claims.
Ask: Who designed the study? Who selected the metrics? Was there a comparison group?
1
Vendor-stated claim / expert opinion
Not evidence
"Clinically grounded." "Evidence-based." "Clinician-approved." These are marketing claims, not evidence. SOC 2 and HIPAA certifications are data security standards — they say nothing about clinical effectiveness. A vendor saying their tool is "built on CBT principles" is not the same as a study showing it delivers CBT outcomes.
This is the evidence level of the overwhelming majority of AI tools currently being sold to behavioral health organizations.

7 questions to ask any vendor

1
Evidence level
Do you have peer-reviewed clinical studies? Was the study design pre-registered before data collection began?
Green: Pre-registered RCT published in clinical journal  ·  Red: "We have a case study from a partner."
2
Clinical theory
Which established psychotherapy modalities does your tool incorporate, and how were they operationalized in the AI model?
Green: Specific modalities named, clinical team involved in design  ·  Red: "Our tool is clinically informed."
3
Escalation design
What happens when your AI flags a patient as at-risk or in crisis? Walk me through the exact escalation pathway step by step.
Green: Documented human-in-loop with defined response time  ·  Red: Vague or dependent on clinician checking the dashboard.
4
Bias testing
Has your model been tested for performance differences across race, ethnicity, age, and diagnostic category? Where can I review those results?
Green: Published disparity analysis  ·  Red: "We trained on a diverse dataset."
5
Accountability
What contractual language covers your organization's liability if an AI-assisted decision contributes to a patient harm event?
Green: Explicit clinical accountability clause  ·  Red: Standard limitation of liability with no clinical carve-out.
6
Data practices
Is session content, PHI, or patient interaction data used to train or improve your models? For which customers, under what terms?
Green: Explicit opt-out or no-training clause in BAA  ·  Red: Training terms not addressed in contract.
7
Outcome measurement
What clinical outcome measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7, PCL-5, or equivalent) does your platform support, and how are those results surfaced to clinical leadership?
Green: Validated MBC measures integrated and reportable  ·  Red: Operational metrics only (notes completed, time saved).

The Vendor
Vetting Rubric

Score any AI tool across five clinical dimensions before it enters a patient pathway. Each dimension is rated 1–5. A total score under 15 is a stop sign. Use this with your clinical leadership team — not alone, and not after you've already signed a contract.

Use this when
You have 2–3 finalists from vendor evaluation and need a structured clinical decision framework before procurement.
Use after Cards 1 and 2. Involve your CMO, CCO, or clinical director.
How to use this rubric: Score each vendor on all five dimensions independently. Have at least two clinical leaders score separately, then compare. Disagreements on individual scores are data — discuss them before averaging. A single dimension score of 1 on Escalation Design or Clinical Accountability is a disqualifier regardless of total score.
